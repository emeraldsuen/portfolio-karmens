typographic space

Typographic Space is an academic project that focuses on the geometric typeface, Avenir. A mock magazine was created that features four different graphical layouts that:

  1. Rely solely on type
  2. Includes typographic elements
  3. Introduces a secondary typeface
  4. Features extreme size and weight contrast

Dieter Rams' 10 Principles for Good Design is the content of these layouts and are reflected in my design.


Roughs

As this project is typography based, working directly on Adobe Illustrator allowed me to quickly adjust placement, sizing, and orientation. The above images are only a small portion of the exploration I have done through my ideation phase.


Mockups

I narrowed down my sketches to four layouts that had potential for further development, fine tuning them for the final compositions. In the first three layouts, I focused on clarity. The last layout took a more conceptual and expressive spin.

Final Layouts

In layout one, I was limited to one font size and two weights. Many of my initial mockups were either symmetrical, or badly balanced. Ultimately, I chose to use a bold typeface for headings and indented the body text to establish hierarchy in the composition. The lower right placement gives the piece a sense of balance and stability.

The second composition required me to include typographic elements that included lines, shapes, or textures. I chose to use a vertical line that runs through the page, with small circles that indicate the start of each principle.

Inspired by Massimo Vignelliâ€™s work, I chose to introduce a large and bold Helvetica paired with a small body text. The line-text-line pattern created a natural, free flowing pattern throughout the layout. I paid close attention to the relationship between heading and body, matching the baselines and x-heights of both chunks of text.

The last piece is my most expressive one in the assignment. Graphic design often appears to have a checklist of elements for successful compositions. I used these elements that are typically categorized as efficient design tools, including a grid structure, asymmetrical balance, and contrast, and created one chaotic and unreadable composition by overworking these tools. The idea originated when fiddling with mockups for layout one and three. The two initial iterations had two different grid structures, with the large headings spanning 68 pixels per column and the small body text sharing a five column grid.



Reflection

Because of their vastly opposing concepts, I believe that the third and fourth layouts are the highlights of Typographic Space. On the one hand, I demonstrated the use of the 10 principles listed in my magazine in layout three, focusing on unobtrusiveness and challenging my attention to small typographic details. On the other hand, the last layout is the antithesis of Dieter Rams' principles.

My final presentation of the four distinct compositions were compiled into a magazine format that includes an introduction, a process documentation, and a design rationale. The magazine is printed on 100lb paper and bound using saddle stitch.

This academic project challenged my attention to detail, such as kerning, leading, and text alignment, carefully on each page of the publication. If I had another chance to redo this project, I would use another typeface for my magazine content, which may be riskier, but it can set apart my four compositions from the entire work.
